Integration by Parts
CALCULUS UNDERGRADUATE TECHNIQUE ~18 min · Generated for you

Integration by parts is one of the most powerful techniques in your calculus toolkit. It lets you integrate products of functions — the kind of integral that substitution can't touch. Whenever you see something like ∫x·eˣ dx or ∫x·sin(x) dx, this is your move.

Core Formula
∫ u dv = uv − ∫ v du
This comes directly from the product rule for derivatives. If you can identify u and dv from your integrand, you can transform a hard integral into a (hopefully) simpler one.
Choosing u and dv — The LIATE Rule

The hardest part is knowing what to call u and what to call dv. The LIATE mnemonic gives you a priority order for choosing u:

LIATE Priority — Choose u from the top
L — Logarithmic: ln(x), log(x)
I — Inverse trig: arctan(x), arcsin(x)
A — Algebraic: x², x³, polynomials
T — Trig: sin(x), cos(x), tan(x)
E — Exponential: eˣ, 2ˣ (usually dv)
Worked Example

Let's solve ∫ x·eˣ dx step by step. This is the classic integration by parts problem.

Step-by-step solution
1
Identify u and dv
u = x → du = dx
dv = eˣ dx → v = eˣ
2
Apply the formula: ∫ u dv = uv − ∫ v du
∫ x·eˣ dx = x·eˣ − ∫ eˣ dx
3
Evaluate the remaining integral
= x·eˣ − eˣ + C = eˣ(x − 1) + C ✓

Think of integration by parts as a trade. You're trading a complex integral for a hopefully simpler one. You pick one part of your product to "use up" (u) and another to "integrate" (dv). After the trade, you have a new integral to deal with — but it should be easier. Sometimes you have to do the trade twice.

Practice Problem
Try it yourself
Evaluate: ∫ x·cos(x) dx
A x·sin(x) + C
B x·sin(x) + cos(x) + C
C x·cos(x) − sin(x) + C
D −x·cos(x) + sin(x) + C
✓ Correct! Let u = x (du = dx) and dv = cos(x)dx (v = sin(x)). Applying ∫u dv = uv − ∫v du gives x·sin(x) − ∫sin(x)dx = x·sin(x) + cos(x) + C.
