Synthesized Answer · Lucidia
91% confidence · 5 primary sources
CRISPR-Cas9 is a gene-editing tool derived from a bacterial immune system. It uses a guide RNA to direct the Cas9 protein to a precise location in the genome, where it cuts the DNA double strand. The cell's own repair mechanisms then fix the break — either disabling a gene (knockout) or inserting new genetic material (knock-in).

The key components: a guide RNA (gRNA) that matches your target sequence, and the Cas9 endonuclease that does the cutting. The guide RNA is the programmable part — change it, and you change the target.
