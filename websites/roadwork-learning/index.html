Unit 2 · Forces · Lesson 06
Newton's Third Law
~15 min · Grade 11 · Generated for Alex
Core Concept
generated for your learning style
FA→B = −FB→A
Every action force has an equal and opposite reaction force.
The forces act on different objects — they never cancel each other.
🚀
Action
Rocket pushes gas backward with force F.
↔
Reaction
Gas pushes rocket forward with equal force F.
🎯
Key Insight
Different objects. Same magnitude. Opposite direction.
Newton's Third Law says: for every force, there is an equal and opposite force on a different object. When you push a wall, the wall pushes back on you with the exact same amount of force — you just don't move it because it's anchored to the Earth.
Gaming analogy · chosen for you
Think of it like a fighting game hitbox collision. When your character's punch lands, the game applies damage to your opponent — but your hand also registers the collision force, which is why you can't phase through walls. The engine applies equal opposing force vectors on both hitboxes simultaneously. Newton's Third Law is literally baked into every physics engine.

The tricky part is understanding why action-reaction pairs don't cancel out. They act on different objects. When Skater A pushes Skater B, the reaction force acts on A — not B. So each skater accelerates in opposite directions. They can't cancel because they're not on the same object.
Quick check: Two ice skaters push off each other. Skater A has twice the mass of Skater B. Which statement is true about the forces?
A
Skater A pushes B with more force because A is heavier.
B
Both skaters push each other with the exact same force magnitude.
C
Skater B pushes A with more force as a reaction.
D
There's no reaction force because A initiated the push.
Correct! Newton's Third Law says equal magnitude regardless of mass. Mass affects acceleration (F=ma) — not the force pair. Skater B accelerates more because B is lighter, not because B receives more force.
Not quite. The force pair is always equal in magnitude — mass doesn't change the forces. Try again: which property does mass actually affect?
